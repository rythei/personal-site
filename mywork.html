    Over the Summer of 2017, I was hired to work as an operations intern at Amazon.com in Seattle, Washington. Using my background in statistics and econometrics, I designed statistical models to analyze a program of remote customer service agents.     The Luminosity Lab

    Entering my Senior year at ASU, I was hired as a research associate in ASU's Luminosity Lab. Luminosity is comprised of a team of top graduate and undergraduate students from a variety of fields, with the goal of developing new technologies, specifically in the area of education. Projects include the development of autonomous drones for safety escort, the education-competition website Mindspark.io, and scalable smart-mirror technology.

    For Mindspark, I developed a peer-ranking algorithm that allows users who enter competitions to judge their peers and be ranked on certain skills. My algorithm uses topological sorting technique and a generalization of the ELO ranking system called TrueSkill. The repository for my code implementing this ranking scheme can be found here.

    Another project I have led at Luminosity is the development of an Income Share Agreement scheme for student funding. The idea is to use equity investment in students' educations rather than debt investment (i.e. traditional student loans). A short paper I wrote describing the idea can be found here. The proposed program is currently under consideration by the University Administration.

    As a graduate student, I will continue my work with the Luminosity Lab, leading a team of machine learning specialists on various research projects.

    MCTP Research Experience for Undergraduates

    During the summer of 2015, I recieved funding as a part of an NSF Research Experience for Undergraduates (called MCTP) to work on an applied mathematics research project with Dr. Sebastien Motsch and fellow undergraduate Alex Reamy at ASU. Our work focused primarily on an extension of model originally derived by mathematicians Cucker and Smale to describe the dynamics of flocking and swarming behaviors in nature. The model is defined formally as a system of ordinary differential equations (though we ultimately expanded the model to its analogous PDE and kinetic form), and is designed to have the following basic properties: agents that are close to each other tend to repel, agents that are far from each other tend to attract, and agents tend to align their velocities to those of their neighbors. The relative intensity of these forces is determined by a defined "influence function".

    The main aim of our research was to determine a sufficient condition that would guarantee the system's convergence to some form of "flocking" behavior. Indeed, we were able to prove that, given a particular property of the influence function, the system would converge to a "flock".
