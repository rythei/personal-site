    Interactive map for 2017 ASU GSV Summit

    D3.js
    Data Visualization
    Data Mining
    Every year, Arizona State University partners with Global Silicon Valley (GSV) to host one of the world's largest Education Technology summits in world - attendees and speakers have included Bill Gates, Sal Khan, and Condoleezza Rice. In preparation for the 2017 Summit, I was asked to build a visualization illustrating the attendees and their many connections throughout the world of ed-tech. To do this, I designed and built a map in D3.js with each of the Summit's attendees (mostly ed-tech companies). Using a list of companies and individuals attending, I scraped the web for information on where in the world each party was based out of, as well as any information that could be garnered on their work, business size, and connections to one another. This information is then aggregated into an interactive map. A live version of the map is currently hosted at https://rythei.github.io/ASU-GSV-Map/.

    Peer-ranking algorithm for Mindspark.io

    Python
    Django.db
    Algorithm Design

    Mindspark.io is a competition websites where students can access real-world problems provided by companies. The idea is that students at universities often graduate having little knowledge of what types of problems are encountered in industry, and hence little experience solving such problems. Mindspark attempts to bridge this gap by offering a space where students can compete in teams to solve problems inspired by those that they might encounter after graduation.

    One of the main features that we wanted the site to have was the ability to rank individuals on particular skills (say, web development or data visualization) based on their submissions, so that potential employers could use this information to help make hiring decisions from the website. Ultimately we decided that the best means to implement this was through a peer ranking system: students would rank other students submissions based on a pre-defined set of criteria. The remaining questions was how to do this without creating adverse incentives for students to rate others intentionally poorly. My solution is as follows: for each submission, the submitting student must "peer rank" three other submissions - from best to worst - before his/her own can be considered. After this, my algorithm aggregates the groups of 3 sub-rankings into an ordered ranking of the entire population. From here, I update global rankings of each individual who submitted a project using a generalization of the ELO ranking system called TrueSkill. The resulting system requires an individual to compete in an approximately four competitions before the algorithm begins to reflect the true rankings of individuals.

    The repository for my code implementing this ranking scheme can be found here. To learn more about Mindspark, or to sign up to host or compete in a competition, visit Mindspark.io.

    A three-zone model of flocking behavior

    Applied Mathematics
    Research

    During the summer of 2015, I recieved funding as a part of an NSF Research Experience for Undergraduates (called MCTP) to work on an applied mathematics research project with Dr. Sebastien Motsch and fellow undergraduate Alex Reamy at ASU. Our work focused primarily on an extension of model originally derived by mathematicians Cucker and Smale to describe the dynamics of flocking and swarming behaviors in nature. The model is defined formally as a system of ordinary differential equations (though we ultimately expanded the model to its analogous PDE and kinetic form), and is designed to have the following basic properties: agents that are close to each other tend to repel, agents that are far from each other tend to attract, and agents tend to align their velocities to those of their neighbors. The relative intensity of these forces is determined by a defined "influence function".

    The main aim of our research was to determine a sufficient condition that would guarantee the system's convergence to some form of "flocking" behavior. Indeed, we were able to prove that, given a particular property of the influence function, the system would converge to a "flock".
